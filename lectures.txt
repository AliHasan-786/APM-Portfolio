What is Trust & Safety?

A practitioner’s definition of Trust & Safety

“the part of a digital service’s operations that focuses on
understanding and addressing the harmful content or
conduct associated with that service”
(Digital Trust & Safety Partnership)

An academic’s definition of Trust & Safety

“Trust and safety is the study of how people abuse the internet
to cause real human harm, often using products the way they
are designed to work”
(S. Grossman, Journal of Online Trust & Safety)

A more memorable formulation

“The internet’s erosion of norms as a constraint on human
behavior unleashed a flood of speech in the lawful-but-awful
category: material that cannot be prohibited by law but that
profoundly violates many people’s sense of decency, morality,
or justice.”
(D. Keller, Stanford Cyber Policy Center)

Trust & Safety responsibilities
ANTICIPATE HARMS
> Run risk assessments for new products or features (for example, through red teaming or structured
evals)
> Study rising dangerous trends on similar products and in society as a whole (for example, by
monitoring extremist groups on Telegram for new hate terms)

INCENTIVIZE SAFE EXPERIENCES
> Help build classifiers and other mechanisms to downrank harmful content and promote useful
material (for example, Google Search’s “EAT” framework)
> Design, test and launch interventions that might reduce the likelihood of users engaging in harmful
behavior (for example, Twitter’s defunct suggestions to read an article before retweeting)

MITIGATE HARMS
> Define unacceptable content, user and product behaviors (typically through a centralized content
policy)
> Respond to user reports and mandated takedown requests (typically through a flagging mechanism on
individual posts/accounts)

Regulators also increasingly define online harms
Recent laws are requiring platforms to prevent, mitigate, and report on the
state of online harms on their services.
Online Safety Act: Focuses on cyberbullying, adult cyber-abuse, non-consensual
sharing of intimate images, and material depicting abhorrent violent conduct.
Digital Services Act: Covers freedom of expression and pluralism, discrimination,
consumer protection, children’s rights, public security and electoral processes,
gender-based violence, public health, and mental and physical wellbeing.
Online Safety Act: Includes cyberbullying, sexual or violent behaviour and acts,
suicide and self-harm, vice and organised crime, products/acts that endanger public
health.
Online Safety Act: Covers CSAM, violence, facilitating suicide, fraud, and terrorism.
Might expand to crisis response protocols

Harassment: 1-to-1 or many-on-1 emotional abuse aimed at scaring, embarrassing or
humiliating its target
Unwanted sexual content: dissemination or exposure of sexually explicit content in a way
that is undesired by the recipient or the subject of the material

Violence, hate, and terrorism: wanton dissemination or glorification of material that is

from one source when it is actually a concealed effort to influence public opinion
Bias: explicit or implicit preference for a protected demographic group or other sensitive

Trust & Safety risk assessments
New product features go through a range of reviews (privacy, legal). At large content
platforms that typically includes safety reviews (more or less formalized).
Methods:
● Structured evaluations (“evals”) against existing policy and known user behaviors
● Threat intelligence and crisis scenario planning
● Adversarial red teaming
Outcomes:
● Updates in product design
● New safety guardrails
● Delaying or even canceling launch

Structured evals
Conducted on an internal version of the tool or a small pilot (e.g. <1% of users)

Representative set:
For ~1,000 queries/posts/interactions that are typical user behavior, assess the % of policy
violations and new risks
Adversarial set:
For ~1,000 queries/posts/interactions that are known harmful patterns, assess the % of policy
violations and new risks

Levers

1. Labeling actors, topics, or content as potentially risky
2. Nudging (or sludging) users to behave differently
3. Raising authoritative content to fight “bad speech with good speech”
4. Reducing reach of borderline content or content by known bad actors
5. Delegating moderation decisions to users and groups

Labeling (actors)
Intervention: Provide more
context to users about the
author of a post
Infrastructure: Consistent
definitions, strong detection
mechanisms for related
accounts, and/or self-labeling
options.
Challenges: Definitions are
slippery. Do viewers notice /
understand what is being
presented?

Labeling (content)
Extremely popular.
Intervention: Warn or blur at the
content level and describe why.
Infrastructure: Classifiers that
successfully recognize the type of
content. Capable internal/external
flaggers. User affordances that
describe label (and persist).
Challenges: This is also a
moderation exercise. Labels don’t
carry on re-uploads. Errors are
visible. May be passing the buck to
third parties.

Labeling (topics)
Intervention: Provides helpful
tips/support that goes beyond
debating individual content

Infrastructure: Keyword
detection; source quality
scoring.
Challenges: Over-triggering
may spook or annoy users,
partner organizations must be
well-staffed.

Nudging or sludging users
Intervention: Encourage
users to avoid a harmful
behavior (or engage in a
positive one)
Infrastructure: Keyword
detection; source quality
scoring.
Challenges: Intervention
can be patronizing and feel
creepy.

Raising high quality sources
Intervention: Highlight
accurate sources to serve as
counter to possible
misinformation.
Infrastructure: Keyword
detection; source quality
scoring.

Challenges: Highly curated
and interventionist approach.
Requires a commitment to
withstand attacks for calling a
source authoritative.

Reducing reach
Intervention: Cease
recommending harmful
content – or actively depress
its future reach – without
removing it.
Infrastructure: Definition,
labeling, and detection
mechanisms for “borderline”
content.
Challenges: Proximity to
policy violation can be
subjective; feeds into concerns
about unfair “shadowbanning.”

Empowering users and community moderators
Intervention: Give
users (or creators)
delegated authority
over moderation.
Infrastructure:
channel or accountlevel rules engines
and moderation tools.
Challenges: Can
contradict your own
moderation goals and
“industrialize” some of
the potential harms.

Simple approaches used widely:
- Lists of content (exact matching known-bad content)
- Can use cryptographic hash function H
H(content) = H(content’) iff content = content’
- Regular expressions ( “hate.” or “hate(r|s)” )
- For text content, doesn’t work for images

For rich media data, need other approaches to similarity matching:
- PhotoDNA, PHash, PDQhash
- Similarity hash function H s.t.:
|H(image) – H(image’)| < threshold
if image, image’ very similar

Solution? Block home addresses
Policy: BanalityBook will no longer allow residential
addresses to be appended to posts. When we suspect
that the location is tied to a home address, we will
delete the location.

●

Oak Street, Apartment 4B") in location tags
Allowed: Description of proximity to landmarks (e.g. “I

identifiers or overall zip code; historical or fictional
residential addresses (e.g. Truman Capote’s House at

Enforcement mechanism: A custom classifier will strip

“Traps” in abstracting social problems to technical ones
The framing trap

Failure to model the entire system over which a social criterion, such as
fairness, will be enforced

The portability trap

Failure to understand how repurposing algorithmic solutions designed for
one social context may be misleading, inaccurate, or otherwise do harm
when applied to a different context

The formalism trap

Failure to account for the full meaning of social concepts such as fairness,
which can be procedural, contextual, and contestable, and cannot be
resolved through mathematical formalisms

The ripple trap

Failure to understand how the insertion of technology into an existing social
system changes the behaviors and embedded values of the pre-existing
system

The solutionism trap

Failure to recognize the possibility that the best solution to a problem may
not involve technology

https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/3287560.3287598

Emotional labor
“trying to feel the right feeling for a job, either by evoking or suppressing feelings.” - Wikipedia
Coined by Hothschild in 1983 book “The Managed Heart”:
● Investigated through lens of service workers (flight attendants, bill collectors)
● Cognitive, bodily, expressive mechanisms for emotional regulation strategies

Emotional labor often unrecognized and unaccounted for

Burnout
Physical and emotional exhaustion that workers can experience when they have low
job satisfaction and feel powerless and overwhelmed at work
Contributing factors:
● feeling either constantly overworked or under-challenged,
● being under time pressure all the time,
● having conflicts with colleagues, and
● extreme commitment with people neglecting their own needs.

Symptoms
● exhaustion
● alienation from work-related activities
● reduced performance

Trauma
Results from experiences that are physically or emotionally harmful with lasting adverse
effects on the ability to function and mental, physical, social, emotional well-being
(SAMHSA)
Studies suggest >68% of people experience trauma at some point in their lives
People experience trauma differently

Trauma can cause emotional, neurological, behavioral changes

Trauma stress reactions
Human survival instincts developed in
response to the traumatic event(s)
Physical (trouble sleeping, hyperarousal, …)
Emotional (anxiety, severe mood swings, …)
Behavioral (avoidance, high-risk activities, …)
Cognitive (hypervigilance, intrusive thoughts, …)
Existentially (hopelessness, confidence)

Secondary trauma (aka vicarious trauma)
Acute response to being exposed to someone else’s traumatic experience

Negative
● burnout
● 'compassion fatigue'
● inability to concentrate
● avoidance
● lack of joy
● physical issues (lack of appetite,
sleep issues)
● irritability
● sadness or numbness
● 'savior'/lack of boundaries

Neutral/Positive
● vicarious resilience
● compassion satisfaction
● engagement
● gratitude and appreciation
● 'switch off'
● boundaries
● prioritizing self and needs
● experience sharing

Strategies for vicarious trauma
[McCann & Pearlman 1990]:
● Striving for balance between our personal and professional lives;
● Balancing a clinical caseload with other professional involvements such as research
and teaching that can replenish us;
● Balancing victim with non-victim cases;
● Being aware of and respecting our own personal boundaries, such as limiting evening
or weekend work;
● Developing realistic expectations of ourselves in doing this type of work;
● Giving ourselves permission to experience fully any emotional reactions of which we
are aware;
● Finding ways to nurture and support ourselves;
● Engaging in political work for social change;
● Seeking out non-victim-related activities that provide hope and optimism

Medeiros and Prochaska (1988): "optimistic perseverance”
